Executive Summary

Cambridge's FY2026 adopted operating budget is $992M — a 23.8% increase from FY2024's $802M. Education is the largest service at $280M. The city raises $992M in revenue, 72% of which ($712M) comes from property taxes. The 7-year capital plan (FY2024–2030) totals $1020M. The FY2026 property tax base is $304.6B — anchored by Harvard ($137B) and MIT ($71B), both mostly tax-exempt.

Operating Budget — Full Picture (FY2011–FY2026)

The city's adopted operating budget spans all services — Education, Employee Benefits, Debt Service, Public Safety, Human Services, and more. This is the complete $992M picture, not the salary-only subset.

Budget vs. payroll: The position-level salary data ($380M) represents authorized base pay for filled positions. The operating budget's "Salaries & Wages" line ($618M) also includes overtime, benefits, and positions not published in the salary dataset. The remaining $374M covers debt service, MWRA payments, Cherry Sheet (state assessments), supplies, and other ordinary maintenance.

Operating Revenue — FY2026 Sources

Cambridge raises $992M to fund its operating budget. Property taxes alone ($712M, 72%) make Cambridge one of the most property-tax-dependent cities in Massachusetts, driven by its exceptional tax base anchored by Harvard and MIT.

Why Cambridge is tax-rich: Harvard's $137B and MIT's $71B in assessed property pay voluntary PILOT (Payment in Lieu of Taxes) agreements — well below what full tax liability would be. Yet Cambridge still generates $712M in property taxes because of its dense commercial and residential tax base. Per-capita property tax revenue in Cambridge is among the highest of any Massachusetts municipality.

Property Tax Base — FY2026 Assessment ($304.6B)

Cambridge's exceptional property tax base — the densest outside of Boston — drives 72% of city revenue. The largest assessed properties are institutional, mostly exempt.

Harvard University ($137B, 335 parcels) and MIT ($71B, 194 parcels) together hold $208B in assessed value — 68% of the city's total — most of which is tax-exempt under M.G.L. c. 59 §5. Both universities negotiate voluntary PILOT agreements. At the FY2026 residential rate of $5.86/$1,000, full liability on Harvard's holdings alone would be approximately $800M annually — nearly the entire city operating budget.

Methodology & Data Limitations

  • Operating budget & revenue (5bn4-5wey, ixyv-mje6): The Cambridge Open Budget portal exposes the city's adopted operating budget as two parallel datasets — expenditures by service/department/category and revenues by type. Both represent the adopted budget, not actual spending or collections. FY2011–2026 data available.
  • Capital budget (9chi-2ed3): The 7-year capital improvement plan with approved appropriations by project and year. Includes both current year (FY2026) and forward projections (FY2027–2030) which are plans, not appropriations.
  • Salary data (multiple datasets): Position-level budget showing authorized base salary per position. Does not include individual employee names (unlike MA state CTHRU). FY2025 is incomplete (Education missing). The "Salaries & Wages" line in the operating budget ($618M) exceeds the position-level salary total ($380M) because it includes overtime, benefits, positions not in the open dataset, and elected officials.
  • Contracts (gp98-ja4f): Contract registry without dollar values. ~41 "TBD" vendor entries excluded from vendor analysis.
  • Property (waa7-ibdu): FY2026 assessed values from Cambridge Assessing Dept. Tax-exempt status is not directly flagged — requires cross-referencing the Assessor's exemption records.
